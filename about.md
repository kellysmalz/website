My path to academia was not linear. After attending several undergraduate institutions and stepping away from higher education for a period, I ultimately completed my degree at a university where faculty were approachable, cultivated curiosity, and recognized my potential before I did. Their mentorship changed the trajectory of my life, and it is the model I carry into every classroom. I became an educator because someone invested in me; I teach the way I do because I believe every student deserves that same experience.

I am a Preceptor of Data Science at the University of Chicago Data Science Institute, where I develop and implement curricular content for data science and mathematics courses at UChicago and the City Colleges of Chicago. I mentor students through capstone projects (The Clinic), Data Science for Social Impact collaborations, and applied industry initiatives, and I serve on the graduate admissions committee.

My research sits at the intersection of probabilistic numerical methods, machine learning, and education. I hold a Ph.D. in Applied Mathematics from the University of North Carolina at Charlotte — dissertation: *Probabilistic Numerical Integration with Applications in Machine Learning* — and a Data Science and Machine Learning Certificate from MIT.

### Teaching

My teaching philosophy is grounded in inclusivity, practical relevance, collaboration, growth, and flexibility. I have built courses and curricula across a wide range of institutional contexts: a private R1 (University of Chicago), a community college system (City Colleges of Chicago), a liberal arts college (Belmont Abbey College), and a large public R1 (University of North Carolina at Charlotte). Each setting has shaped how I think about instruction, from supporting students with no prior coding experience to guiding graduate students through the full data science workflow on real-world problems.

An inclusive classroom is especially important in mathematics and data science, where students arrive with dramatically different preparation — in programming, in statistics, in mathematics, and in comfort with ambiguity. I begin every new topic by surfacing what students already know and addressing gaps before introducing new complexity. These moments normalize confusion, activate prior knowledge, build peer relationships, and shift the locus of sense-making from me to the students. I use frequent check-ins, short reflection prompts, and informal polls throughout class to gauge understanding in real time and catch students who are quietly falling behind before the gap becomes unmanageable.

Data science is a discipline learned by doing, and I structure my courses accordingly. I model logical reasoning aloud — including the dead ends and the moments of uncertainty — and then progressively transfer explanatory responsibility to students. Rather than watching me work through a problem, students manipulate real data, test hypotheses, compare approaches, and defend their choices. I prioritize real, contemporary datasets and draw on examples that connect analytical methods to consequential questions: how resources are distributed, who benefits from policy decisions, and where systems fall short. My goal is not just technical competence; it is analytical agency — the confidence to approach an unfamiliar problem, break it down, and reason through it in a way that is both rigorous and honest about its limitations.

I have extended this philosophy beyond the classroom by mentoring students through The Clinic and the Data Science for Social Impact program, where they collaborate directly with external partners on applied data problems. Projects I have mentored include approximating the value and irreplaceability of rare books at the Regenstein Library, building a data dashboard for Mothers Out Front to help activists advance climate solutions and advocate for children's health, and partnering with RAFI-USA to expose inequitable access to food and corporate concentration in grocery markets across the United States. I treat applied mentorship as a natural extension of teaching, and it is some of the most meaningful work I do.

I have taught across a wide range of institutions and levels: K–12, introductory, and upper-level data science at UChicago; introductory and advanced mathematics and statistics at the City Colleges of Chicago, Belmont Abbey College, and UNC Charlotte.

### Service

Strong departments and institutions are built through collective effort. My service strengths lie in student mentorship and admissions — both rooted in my own undergraduate experience, where faculty guidance was transformative.

I have mentored students in NSF-funded undergraduate research, The Clinic (senior capstone), and the Data Science for Social Impact program at UChicago. I have served on hiring, admissions, strategic planning, and promotion and tenure committees across institutions, and served as Chair of the Department of Mathematics and Physics at Belmont Abbey College. I have been an NSF panelist (2023, 2024) and served on program committees for AIED, PME-NA, and LOD.

I am a member of the Board of Directors of Accessible Math (501(c)(3)) and a member of Pi Mu Epsilon, the American Mathematical Society, and the Mathematical Association of America. I volunteer with the Chicago Food Depository and serve as a class volunteer at the UChicago Laboratory Schools.
